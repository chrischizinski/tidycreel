## Introduction
Brief framing: this vignette explains how interview data becomes a catch rate estimate and then a total catch estimate. Audience is a creel biologist who knows what catch rate means but not ratio estimators or delta method. State both will be explained from first principles.

## From Interviews to Catch Rate
Explain what interview data contains: for each intercepted angler, we record trip duration and catch. Catch rate (CPUE) is catch per angler-hour. The challenge: we don't observe the full population, only a sample of anglers who happened to be present at count locations.

Two approaches exist for estimating mean CPUE from a sample:

### Ratio-of-Means (ROM)
Show the ROM formula:
$$\hat{R}_{\text{ROM}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} h_i}$$
where $c_i$ = catch of angler $i$ and $h_i$ = trip hours of angler $i$. Gloss symbols. Plain-language interpretation: "Total catch in sample ÷ total hours in sample."

### Mean-of-Ratios (MOR)
Show the MOR formula:
$$\hat{R}_{\text{MOR}} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{c_i}{h_i}$$
Gloss symbols. Plain-language interpretation: "Average of each angler's individual catch rate."

### Side-by-Side Comparison
Construct a small inline data frame: 5 anglers, round numbers for catch and hours. Compute ROM and MOR by hand (show the arithmetic), print the result. Then confirm both with an R code block using estimate_catch_rate(method = "ROM") and estimate_catch_rate(method = "MOR") on the same data.

After showing the results, explain when each is appropriate:
- ROM is appropriate when angler trip durations are highly variable (longer trips dominate the total hours; ROM correctly down-weights short trips)
- MOR is appropriate when each angler is considered an independent, equally-weighted observation
- ROM is the default in tidycreel for instantaneous-count surveys following Pollock et al. (1994)

## From Catch Rate to Total Catch
Explain that total catch is the product: $\hat{TC} = \hat{E} \times \hat{R}$, where $\hat{E}$ is estimated effort and $\hat{R}$ is the catch rate. Both are estimated with uncertainty, so the product's variance requires special treatment.

### Delta Method Variance
Introduce the delta method in one intuitive paragraph: "For a product of two estimated quantities, the variance of the product can be approximated by linearizing around the estimates — treating each as fixed and asking how much the product would change if each varied by its standard error."

Show the decomposed variance formula:
$$\text{Var}(\hat{TC}) \approx \hat{E}^2 \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{R}) + \hat{R}^2 \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{E}) + 2\hat{E}\hat{R} \cdot \text{Cov}(\hat{E}, \hat{R})$$

Annotate each of the three terms:
- $\hat{E}^2 \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{R})$: "How much of the total-catch uncertainty comes from imprecise catch-rate estimation (effort treated as known)"
- $\hat{R}^2 \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{E})$: "How much comes from imprecise effort estimation (catch rate treated as known)"
- Cross-term: "Covariance adjustment — usually small; tidycreel assumes zero covariance between effort and catch-rate samples, so this term vanishes"

After the formula, show a worked numeric: use the effort estimate from the single-count example (from the Introduction paragraph — reference back to it; use round numbers) and the ROM catch rate from the side-by-side comparison. Compute each term by hand. Confirm with estimate_total_catch() on the same combined data.

## Summary
One paragraph: interview data → ROM or MOR catch rate → delta method propagates effort and CPUE uncertainty into total catch. Remind reader which estimator to default to and why.
